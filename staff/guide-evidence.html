When you need to check evidence

  • If an applicant is claiming that they’re receiving benefits but they’ve not provided their National Insurance number
  • If the application you’re processing is marked for further investigation

In all of these cases, you’ll be provided with a letter to send to the applicant asking them to send their evidence in.


What to do

When evidence is received:
  • select the application from the list ‘Awaiting evidence’
  • select 'Process evidence'
  • calculate the applicant's total monthly income
  • enter the total in the field on the screen
  • get the result and follow the next steps

When no evidence is received after 7 calendar days:
  • select the application from the list ‘Awaiting evidence'
  • select 'write letter to the applicant' and a letter will be generated for you
  • post the letter and all the documents back to the applicant


How to calculate total monthly income

  • add up the totals on the payslips and other documents
  • check this against the bank statement


What not to include as income

  • the 6 eligible benefits
  • the excluded benefits


Sources of evidence

Bank statements

The applicant will be asked to provide the most recent month’s bank statement. Check the money coming in matches the amounts shown on the supporting evidence.

Wages

Employed: Applicants should provide their most recent payslip which should be no more than 6 weeks old.

Self employed: Applicants should provide their most recent tax return (Self Assessment) and HMRC Self Assessment Tax Calculation.

If a self-employed applicant doesn’t have either of these documents, they will need to provide bank statements that show income for the previous tax year.

Child Benefit

Working Tax Credit

Child Tax Credit

A letter from HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) dated in the last 3 months confirming that the applicant is receiving Child Benefit, Working Tax Credit or Child Tax Credit.

In some cases an applicant may not have a Child Benefit Award Notice dated within the last three months. If this is the case, check their bank statements for evidence of money coming in.

Child maintenance payments

Document(s) showing the payment amounts and how often they are made. This can be one of the following:

  • sealed court order
  • Child Support Agency Assessment
  • signed voluntary agreement or letter of agreement signed by both parties

Contribution-based Jobseeker’s Allowance (JSA)

Contribution-based Employment and Support Allowance (ESA)

Universal Credit

A letter from the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) dated in the last 3 months.

Any other benefits

A letter dated in the last 3 months confirming how much is being received and how often.

Note that the 6 eligible benefits and the excluded benefits shouldn’t be included as income.

Pensions

A letter or statement showing pension payments.

Rent from people living with the applicant

A letter dated within the last month from the person paying money to confirm the arrangement and amount paid.

Rent from any properties owned

Tenancy agreement or lease relating to each property showing the rents currently being paid and how often.

Stocks, shares, bonds, savings and/or any other financial arrangements

Current statements or certificates showing the arrangement and how much income is obtained. Only the interest paid out on stocks, bonds, shares, savings etc can be considered as income.

Income received by any other means such as selling goods publicly or privately, including sales over the internet

Official documentary evidence showing the amount of income received, by what method and how often. Note that money received from the sale of capital assets (e.g. family heirlooms), would normally be classified as ‘savings and investments’ rather than income.

Prisoner’s income

Prisoners should provide a Prisoner Income and Expenditure Statement (PIES). This shows the amount of their earnings, spending, private cash and savings.

If the prisoner is in a private prison the format of their financial statement may be slightly different. You need to check details of earnings and private cash.


Evidence in languages other than English

Evidence must be supplied in English. However, Welsh courts and tribunals can also accept evidence in Welsh.

If the original evidence is in another language, then it must be provided along with a certified translation.
